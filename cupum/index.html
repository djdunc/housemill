The Problem - A National Treasure at Risk

  • Who: The House Mill, the UK's largest surviving tidal mill (Grade I listed).
  • What: It faces a growing threat from flooding due to climate change and urban development.
  • The Challenge: Floods happen at all hours, but monitoring relies on volunteers during the day. The true extent of water damage to the historic timber structure was unknown.

The Old Way vs. The New Way

  • Traditional Methods:
    • Infrequent manual checks by a shrinking team of volunteers.
    • Data from regional sensors was not specific enough for the mill's unique location.
    • Reliance on observing the aftermath of floods.
  • Our Solution: A micro-scale, on-site Internet of Things (IoT) monitoring system.

The IoT System in Action

  • What it is: A low-cost system using a sonar sensor and IR camera controlled by a Raspberry Pi.
  • What it does:
    • Measures water height every 10 minutes, 24/7.
    • Transmits data in real-time.
    • Visualizes the findings on a live dashboard for volunteers and stakeholders.

Key Finding #1 - The Hidden Floods Revealed

  • Over 12 months, volunteers could see evidence of 42 floods.
  • The IoT system detected 136 flood events where water touched the floorboards.
  • This is a threefold increase, revealing a much higher frequency of water exposure than previously understood.

Key Finding #2 - Quantifying Submersion Time

  • For the first time, we could measure the exact duration the timber was soaking in water.
  • Example: In October, the floorboards were submerged for 900 minutes (15 hours).
  • The average flood event lasted 53 minutes. This data is crucial for assessing long-term decay risk.

Turning Data into Actionable Insight

  • The key metric became "total time touched by water", a direct request from stakeholders.
  • The data confirmed that over 60% of floods occurred outside of normal working hours (9 am - 6 pm), explaining why so many were missed.
  • This provides a solid evidence base for future conservation and funding requests.

Empowering Volunteers & The Community

  • The system automates a critical monitoring task, reducing the burden on the small volunteer team.
  • Real-time data gives them actionable insights to better protect the site.
  • The public dashboard helps engage the community and improve the visitor experience by making the invisible threat visible.

Future Potential & Next Steps

  • Flood Forecasting: Integrate IoT data with tide and weather data to predict flood events.
  • Early Warning System: Develop alerts that allow for proactive, on-site mitigation.
  • Improve a User-Friendly Interface: Simplify the dashboard for non-technical users to maximize engagement.

Conclusion - A Scalable Model for Heritage Protection

  • This project proves that low-cost IoT is highly effective for monitoring at-risk heritage sites.
  • It provides previously unattainable data, turning anecdotal evidence into hard facts.
  • The model is scalable and can be adopted by other cultural sites worldwide to safeguard our shared heritage against the threats of climate change.
